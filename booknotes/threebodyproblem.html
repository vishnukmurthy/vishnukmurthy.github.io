Crime and Punishment - Fyodor Dostoevsky

8/10

Overall, this book was a really fun, and well thought out sci fi read.

The premise is that a secret project by the Chinese government tries to make contact with extraterrestrial intelligence. Once the humans get a response back, a fundamental question is explored, what do we as humans do if contact with aliens is made?

What follows is an epic story showing the different sides of humanity, the factions that are created, and the line between science, dogma, and religion. Peppered throughout are full expositions on computers, artificial intelligence, astrophysics, and micro particles, all of which will satisfy even the nerdiest of sci fi fans.

One of the most interesting parts of this book is that it is set against the backdrop of the Cultural Revolution. Prior to doing any research, my knowledge on the topic was limited, but the translation by Ken Liu is great as there are plenty of footnotes explaining the historical and cultural references made. The characters embody the cultural background so well that since reading this book I’ve wanted to travel to China to understand the different outlooks between China and the USA. This ability to bridge the West and the East is no easy feat, and deserves recognition.

In terms of pace, this book is very similar to the Hunger Games Series. When I read it back in high school, a teacher described it as a “junk read”. I was offended at the time, because I loved the Hunger Games, but now I understand why. The pace of the book is such that there are no breaks or calm sections. The tension build is very good, but also never stops. One thing continually feeds into the next at a pace akin to an action movie.* To relate literature to food, it does feel like you are continually eating desserts and are continually in search of the next sugar rush.

My Thoughts (Filled with Spoilers):

Humans Vs The Environment.

Liu points to the modern incompatibility between humans and the environment. It is an important theme in his book and is one that I’ve thought a lot about in my life. There are no answers provided, but the book highlights the complexities involved in the issue and maybe admitting that there are no easy answers is an important first step in honestly taking on this problem.

Our Desire for God

When contact with the aliens is made, many people begin to treat the aliens as a Lord and someone who can help course correct humanity. Many of these people don’t believe in a traditional god, and it makes me think about the human need for hope and to believe that there is something higher than our minds, which we can use to orient ourselves in this harsh universe.

Appreciation of the Fragility of this Earth

Theoretically, I understand that this life is so fragile, and that while life on other parts of the universe is possible and likely, it is still extremely rare. Conditions have to be met so exactly, and I think we often take this for granted. The novel shows an alternative planet with three suns that causes civilization to go between periods of extinction and thriving as it travels between the gravitational pull of the three suns via the famous mathematical Three Body Problem.

The civilization never knows when the sun will rise or fall, and often goes through periods of extreme heat where everything is enflamed, and periods of extreme cold when everything freezes.

Reading about this parallel universe made me appreciate the delicate balance that exists on our planet a bunch more.

Perspective Based on Class

When the video game is being played to draw the humans into the side of the Adventists, an observation is made by Wang – the Adventists seem to be all people from higher classes who understand the dark side of humanity better than the common person. When I read this I thought about a best books to read list that had a comparison between a list selected by authors and one selected by readers. The former had A Brave New World high up on the list, while the later had 1984. I will look to find that list.
